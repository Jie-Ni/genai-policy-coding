## Abstract

Whether university generative-AI policies have converged globally since the public release of ChatGPT is an open empirical question. We built a corpus of 64 universities across North America, Europe, East Asia, and Latin America in nine languages, coded 338 policy chunks against a pre-registered eight-theme codebook using two open-weight large language models (Qwen3.6-27B and Mistral-Small-3.2-24B) with schema-constrained JSON decoding, and validated the codings against a hand-coded gold-standard subset. Cross-coder agreement was substantial to near-perfect (mean Cohen's κ = 0.83). The four-region by eight-theme contingency table rejects independence at χ²(24) = 62.54, p = 2.78×10⁻⁵, Cramer's V = 0.349. A pre-registered Cochran-Armitage trend on vendor governance returns Z = 4.07, p = 4.7×10⁻⁵ for the ordered sequence NA > EU > EA > LA. Holm-corrected pairwise z-tests reject the null for the two largest gaps (T7 NA-LA p = 2.7×10⁻⁴; T5 NA-EA p = 1.6×10⁻³). Headline rank-orderings survive 1000 random 80%-institution subsamples and leave-one-region-out. Seven prompt-engineering ablations yield κ ∈ [0.92, 1.00] versus the production baseline. ANOVA and Kruskal-Wallis on sentiment both fail to reject regional equivalence (0/4 use-cases). Regions therefore appear to share a cautiously positive frame for GenAI in teaching while their operational policy content tracks pre-existing institutional concerns: vendor governance in North America, academic integrity in Latin America, pedagogical redesign in East Asia. We propose a two-layer convergence framework extending Bennett (1991) and Drezner (2001) that separates a discursive layer (where global isomorphic pressure produces rapid convergence) from a substantive layer (where infrastructural legacies produce persistent divergence). The methodological contribution is a reproducible open-weight dual-coder pipeline with a pre-registered robustness suite.

## 1. Introduction

ChatGPT's public release in November 2022 confronted universities with a policy problem they had neither anticipated nor collectively resolved. Most large research universities issued at least an interim guideline by mid-2023. By 2025 the open question is no longer whether to write a policy but what shape it should take, how it should be enforced, and at whom its commitments are aimed. A growing empirical literature documents this institutional response. An, Yu, and James (2025) coded the GenAI policies of the top 50 US universities, identifying themes around integrity, disclosure, equity, and teaching redesign, and reported substantial convergence on academic-integrity language. Jin and colleagues (2025) extended the analysis to 40 universities across six regions and found systematic variation in policy framing that an English-only sample could not have surfaced. Crompton and Burke (2023) and Holmes and Tuomi (2022) summarize an emerging international consensus on stated values (responsibility, transparency, accountability) alongside wide variation in operational rules. What remains unstudied at scale is whether the *content* of institutional GenAI policy varies systematically across world regions and language communities, that is, whether universities in Latin America, East Asia, Europe, and North America are converging on a single global template or pursuing distinct policy logics that would be visible in a theme-by-theme comparison.

This paper addresses that gap. We construct a corpus of 64 universities across the four major regions, covering nine auto-detected chunk languages, and code it with two open-weight large language models in parallel under a pre-registered eight-theme codebook. The methodological contribution is a dual-LLM coding protocol with schema-constrained decoding. Its validity rests on substantial cross-coder agreement, on prompt-engineering ablations confirming that the codings are not artifacts of prompt design, and on institution-level resampling tests confirming that the regional comparisons are not artifacts of which 80% of the corpus a reviewer might choose to drop. The substantive contribution is the identification of a North-South gradient on vendor-side institutional governance: 80% of North American institutions, 67% of European, 31% of East Asian, and 15% of Latin American institutions in our sample have issued policy language that commits the institution itself, rather than only its students, to specific governance practices toward GenAI vendors. We argue, drawing on the institutional-logics tradition (Thornton, Ocasio, & Lounsbury, 2012), that this gradient is best understood as a difference in policy entry point rather than a developmental lag: Latin American institutions concentrate on academic integrity (T4 prevalence 92%), East Asian institutions on integration and pedagogical redesign, and North American institutions on a broader portfolio that uniquely emphasizes vendor-side governance and disclosure obligations. The paper proceeds with a theoretical framing (Section 2), methods (Section 3), empirical results (Section 4), discussion (Section 5), limitations (Section 6), and conclusion (Section 7).

## 2. Theoretical Framework

Two complementary traditions in the sociology of organizations frame our hypotheses. The first, institutional isomorphism (DiMaggio & Powell, 1983), predicts that organizations operating in the same field (in our case, the global university) converge on similar structures and policies via three mechanisms: coercive pressure from regulators and accreditors, mimetic copying under uncertainty, and normative pressure from professional networks. Under a strong isomorphism prediction, we would expect the eight policy themes to show similar prevalence across regions, with cross-regional differences accounted for by lag rather than by structural variation. The second tradition, institutional logics (Thornton, Ocasio, & Lounsbury, 2012), predicts the opposite: that universities embedded in distinct national or regional institutional orders (the market-oriented Anglo-American higher-education sector, the state-coordinated European systems, the developmental-state East Asian systems, and the public-mission Latin American systems) will translate the same external shock (the November 2022 release of ChatGPT) through different policy entry points reflecting each order's pre-existing concerns about academic conduct, vendor governance, and equity. Under a strong logics prediction, we would expect substantial cross-regional divergence on themes that are most tied to a particular institutional order: vendor governance (an Anglo-American market-and-procurement concern), academic integrity (a Latin American Catholic-university and continental civil-service concern), and pedagogical redesign (a North American teaching-and-learning-centre concern).

The policy-convergence literature offers a productive middle position. Bennett (1991) and Drezner (2001) distinguish convergence on *goals*, on *content*, and on *style*: policy goals can be globally shared while policy content (the operational rules) and policy style (regulatory tone) diverge by region. Applied to GenAI in higher education, this distinction predicts that we should observe (a) broad agreement that GenAI is integrable into teaching (T1), which is a goal-level convergence, and (b) substantial divergence on the specific operational obligations and the regulatory tone in which they are issued, with regional patterning that mirrors each region's pre-existing institutional logic. Our sentiment-versus-prevalence finding (Section 4.4) is consistent with this framing: regions converge on a cautiously positive teaching sentiment (the goal) while diverging substantively on operational themes (the content). Drezner (2001) emphasizes that the absence of a coercive global authority (such as the WTO is for trade policy) makes content-level convergence in higher education unlikely; the present results extend that intuition to GenAI policy.

Our pre-registered hypotheses, derived from the institutional-logics tradition, were therefore: H1, that T7 vendor governance is most prevalent in NA and least prevalent in LA, reflecting the Anglo-American market-and-procurement logic; H2, that T4 academic integrity is most prevalent in LA, reflecting Catholic-university normative concerns and civil-service-style codified rules; and H3, that sentiment will *not* differ across regions, since the affective frame for emerging technology in higher education is more globally diffused (through international networks, conferences, and English-language journals) than the operational content of policy. All three hypotheses were registered on the OSF preregistration document before any LLM coding was executed.

### 2.1 A theoretical contribution: layered convergence

Beyond applying institutional logics to a new empirical setting, the present paper proposes a small but generalizable theoretical extension of the Bennett-Drezner (1991, 2001) convergence framework. Bennett's typology distinguishes convergence on goals, on content, and on style. Building on the recent special issue on GenAI governance (Taeihagh, 2025) and Besharov and Smith's (2025) AMR Decade Award reflection on multiple institutional logics and organizational hybridity, we argue that this typology collapses what our data show to be analytically distinct *layers*.

We therefore propose a two-layer convergence framework specifically calibrated for transnational educational-technology policy. Layer (a) is a **discursive-tonal layer** comprising sentiment, framing, and the sociotechnical imaginary the policy projects (Linderoth et al., 2024); here, global isomorphic pressure from UNESCO/OECD templates and Anglophone elite universities produces rapid convergence at low symbolic cost. Layer (b) is a **substantive-content layer** comprising specific operational obligations on disclosure, integrity enforcement, vendor governance, equity provision, and assessment redesign; here, divergence persists and tracks each region's pre-existing institutional infrastructures. The most visible illustration is the contrast between the data-and-IP assetization legacies that Komljenovic and colleagues (2024) document in Anglo higher education and the state-developmental coordination apparatus that Jin and colleagues (2025) describe in East Asian higher education.

The mechanism connecting the two layers is *decoupled translation*: institutions adopt the global discursive shell at near-zero cost while their content layer remains anchored to domestic logics. The framework is testable. It predicts that low-cost discursive items (sentiment, normative posture) converge faster than high-cost substantive items (vendor contracts, assessment redesign), and that infrastructural lock-in predicts content divergence better than national culture variables alone. Our sentiment-convergence-versus-content-divergence finding (Section 4.4 vs. Section 4.2) is the first empirical instantiation of this prediction.

### 2.2 Teacher and student perspectives

A purely policy-text analysis risks treating university GenAI policy as if it produced the behaviour it prescribes. The empirical record from 2023–2025 indicates that it does not. Gonsalves (2025) reported that 74% of King's Business School students failed to declare AI use under a no-penalty disclosure policy, with peer normalization and enforcement ambiguity as the dominant drivers rather than policy text. Kim and colleagues (2025), surveying 982 students and 76 faculty at a US research university, documented large disciplinary and gender heterogeneity in attitudes that a cross-national policy comparison ignoring within-country variance will misclassify. Ravšelj and colleagues' (2025) survey of 23,218 students in 109 countries showed that students themselves support regulation but on a different basis (cheating risk) than what most institutional policies emphasize. Bearman, Tai, Dawson, Boud and Ajjawi (2024) articulated the dominant assessment-redesign framework rooted in evaluative-judgement theory; Mishra, Warr and Islam's (2023) TPACK update collapses the content-technology boundary that earlier teacher professional-development frameworks assumed; and Selwyn, Ljungqvist and Sonesson (2025) documented the invisible repair work teachers perform around GenAI's classroom failures, repair work that no policy text in our corpus acknowledges. Holland and Ciachir (2025) observed that on the ground the integrity discourse is more a peer-monitoring problem than a policy-vs-student problem: students judge their own use as legitimate but distrust peers, especially in group work. Equity-as-policy-language and equity-as-outcome also diverge: Zhang, Rice and Wang (2026) found that socio-economic status predicts AI literacy more strongly than general digital literacy, and that ChatGPT use bifurcates into academic-support and academic-displacement patterns moderated by trust. The present paper therefore describes how universities talk about GenAI rather than what they do about it; ethnographic, survey, and assessment-design evidence remains essential. Section 5.2 returns to this gap.

## 3. Methods

### 3.1 Sample and Corpus

We assembled a stratified purposive sample of 64 universities across four regions: North America (NA, n=20), Europe (EU, n=18), East Asia (EA, n=13), and Latin America (LA, n=13). Institutions were drawn from QS-2026 and ARWU-2025 ranking lists with the goal of approximate balance between elite (top-15 per region) and upper (rank 16–40) tiers, and across languages: English, Mandarin Chinese, Japanese, Korean, German, French, Portuguese, and Spanish (and one chunk auto-tagged Afrikaans, §3.1). The pre-registered selection rule required (a) public availability of at least one institution-issued GenAI-related document during 2023–2025 and (b) coverage of at least one document in the institution's primary language.

URL discovery proceeded in two stages, both pre-registered on OSF before any coding ran. Stage one used the Brave Search HTML endpoint with site-restricted queries in each institution's primary language, throttled at 22 seconds per request to respect anti-bot defenses; this yielded seed URLs for eight institutions. Stage two used targeted WebSearch with manual curation for the remaining institutions, selecting only URLs on the institution's own primary or subdomain that pointed to provost, teaching-and-learning, IT-services, or academic-integrity pages. Each surfaced URL was then processed through Trafilatura for HTML extraction and PyMuPDF for PDF extraction, with automated text-quality gates rejecting documents below 200 informative tokens. The auto-detected per-chunk language distribution in the final 338-chunk corpus is: English 70.1% (n=237), Japanese 8.3% (n=28), Portuguese 8.0% (n=27), Spanish 3.8% (n=13), German 3.3% (n=11), Mandarin Chinese 3.3% (n=11), Korean 1.5% (n=5), French 1.5% (n=5), with one chunk auto-tagged by `langdetect` as Afrikaans (0.3%, n=1) that on manual review was a brief mixed-language transliteration block inside an East Asian institution's policy page and was retained without manual relabeling, since the dual-LLM coders process the original chunk text directly rather than relying on the declared language tag.

Documents were chunked into ~500-token windows preserving paragraph boundaries, with a per-institution cap of 12 chunks to prevent over-weighting institutions with very long policy hubs. Each chunk also passed a generative-AI-term gate (any of "ChatGPT", "GenAI", "generative AI", "large language model", or one of 14 non-English equivalents) before being eligible for coding. The final corpus released for coding contained **338 chunks across 64 institutions and eight languages**, distributed across eight balanced shards (26–53 chunks each) for SLURM array execution.

### 3.2 Codebook

We pre-registered an eight-theme codebook designed to capture the multidimensional structure of contemporary GenAI policy: T1 Integration (whether the document positions GenAI as integratable into teaching), T2 Multimodal (coverage of multimodal capabilities beyond text), T3 Privacy (data-protection clauses tied to GenAI use), T4 Academic Integrity (misconduct, citation, and detection rules), T5 Disclosure (mandatory student or faculty disclosure of GenAI use), T6 Equity (digital-divide and access language), T7 Vendor Governance (institution-side contractual or governance commitments to GenAI vendors), and T8 Pedagogical Redesign (recommendations to revise assessment design in light of GenAI). Each chunk was coded 0/1 per theme. In parallel, the codebook captured a four-dimensional sentiment polarity on a −2..+2 scale across assessment, research, teaching, and administration use-cases. The codebook v1.0 with operational definitions, eight worked examples, and the JSON schema is provided as Supplementary File 1.

### 3.3 LLM Coders and Schema-Constrained Decoding

We used two large language models as independent coders to support cross-coder validation. The primary coder was **Qwen3.6-27B** (Qwen Team, 2026) in bfloat16, and the cross-validator was **Mistral-Small-3.2-24B-Instruct-2506** (Mistral AI, 2025); both were served by vLLM 0.19 (Kwon et al., 2023) with guided JSON-Schema decoding to enforce strict adherence to the codebook output structure. We disabled Qwen's reasoning-trace mode (enable_thinking=False) so that the entire output budget could be used for the JSON object. Although Qwen3.6-27B is natively multimodal (text + vision), only its text-only inference path was invoked here: every chunk in our corpus is pure policy text, and no image input was supplied to the coder. The prompt template provided one system message + 16 few-shot worked examples + the target chunk; we set temperature T=0.0 and seed=11 for the primary run to ensure determinism. Coding was executed in 8-shard parallel SLURM arrays on H100 GPUs with `--gpu-memory-utilization 0.90` and 16,384-token context. Total compute budget across the production run plus seven ablation conditions was approximately five H100-hours.

### 3.4 Statistical Analysis

We pre-specified five families of inferential tests. (a) **Chi-square independence** between region (4 levels) and each of eight themes, with Cramer's V as the effect-size complement. (b) **Mixed-effects logistic regression** for each theme, with random intercept by country and fixed effects for region (NA reference) and institutional tier (elite reference); the model was fit by Laplace-approximated penalized likelihood. (c) **Cochran-Armitage trend** test ordering regions as NA > EU > EA > LA, testing the null of no monotonic trend in regional theme prevalence. (d) **Pairwise two-proportion z-tests** for the three highest-effect-size themes (T7, T4, T5), with Bonferroni correction at α/6 = 0.0083 for six regional pairs. (e) **One-way ANOVA** on regional means for each of the four sentiment use-cases. We also computed two-sided p-values from the mixed-logit Wald statistics and reported them without further multiple-testing correction within the per-theme family, since the eight themes are pre-registered as distinct hypotheses.

Three robustness probes were pre-specified. **S1** drew B=1000 random 80% institution subsamples and recomputed every pairwise regional difference, reporting sign-stability rate. **A2** removed each region in turn and re-evaluated the remaining three-region pairwise structure. **B1** ran seven prompt-engineering ablation conditions on a stratified 80-chunk subset (20 per region): the 16-shot baseline (T=0, seed=11) plus 0/4/8 few-shot variants, T=0.3 with seed=11, and T=0 with seeds 22 and 42. We computed Cohen's κ between every ablation condition and the baseline. All p-values, κ values, and effect sizes are reported with their respective 95% confidence intervals (bootstrap B=2000 for κ; Newcombe's method 10, Newcombe 1998, for the proportion-difference intervals). Analysis was executed in Python 3.11 with NumPy and our own implementations of Cochran-Armitage, Newcombe CI, gammainc-based chi-square tail, and Laplace-approximated mixed logistic; we deliberately avoided statsmodels to keep the analysis lightweight and fully audit-able from a single 600-line script.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Cross-Coder Reliability and Human-Expert Validation

The dual-LLM coding protocol produced 338 paired theme-codings across 64 institutions and eight target themes. Across all themes, mean Cohen's κ between Qwen3.6-27B and Mistral-Small-3.2-24B-Instruct-2506 was **0.83** (n=338), with per-theme values of κ=0.78 (T1 integration), κ=0.80 (T2 multimodal), κ=0.93 (T3 privacy), κ=0.82 (T4 integrity), κ=0.91 (T5 disclosure), κ=0.83 (T6 equity), κ=0.74 (T7 vendor governance), and κ=0.81 (T8 pedagogical redesign). Following the Landis and Koch (1977) framework, every theme reached at least the substantial-agreement threshold (κ ≥ 0.61), and three themes (T3, T5, T6) reached near-perfect agreement (κ ≥ 0.81). Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals from B=2000 paired resamples placed every lower bound at or above 0.65, with the tightest interval observed for T3 privacy at [0.88, 0.97] and the widest for T7 vendor governance at [0.65, 0.82]. Figure 2 plots the per-theme bootstrap distribution of κ. (See Figure 1 for the overall study overview that situates the dual-LLM coding pipeline within the broader cross-national corpus → inference workflow.)

Iteration over corpus scaling produced a slight improvement in κ (0.81 at n=49 institutions, 0.83 at n=64), consistent with the expectation that aggregate IRR stabilizes as the paired-chunk count rises. The annotator-level mixed-effects model (Table 3) showed no systematic offset between the two LLMs on any of the eight themes (all p > 0.11 for the Mistral fixed effect with Qwen as reference), supporting the use of either coder as the primary instrument while the dual-coder pairing provides triangulated validation.

**Human-expert validation.** To address the principal critique that LLM-vs-LLM agreement cannot substitute for human gold-standard validation, the corresponding author hand-coded a stratified subset of 40 chunks (10 per region, 8 languages represented) using the same pre-registered codebook applied to the LLM coders. Agreement between the expert human coding and Qwen3.6 yielded a mean Cohen's κ across the eight binary themes of **0.597, which sits in the upper "moderate" band of the Landis-Koch (1977) reference scale (whose substantial threshold is κ ≥ 0.61)**, with per-theme values κ = 0.94 (T8 pedagogical redesign), 0.72 (T4 integrity), 0.70 (T1 integration), 0.66 (T7 vendor governance), 0.61 (T5 disclosure, at the substantial boundary), 0.55 (T6 equity), 0.54 (T3 privacy), and 0.06 (T2 multimodal). T1, T4, T7, and T8 clearly reach substantial agreement, T5 sits at the substantial threshold, and T3/T6 fall in the moderate band. The T2 multimodal disagreement is a codebook-ambiguity finding rather than a coder-quality finding (the expert applied a narrower "explicit multimodal use" criterion while the LLM applied a broader "code or image mention" criterion); we therefore re-coded T2 in the expert subset for sensitivity (§4.5) and confirmed that the headline T7 result is unchanged. For T7 vendor governance, the theme anchoring the headline finding, the human-vs-Qwen κ is 0.66 (95% CI [0.27, 0.92]), above the substantial threshold; the corresponding κ on T8 pedagogical redesign is near-perfect (κ = 0.94). For the ordinal sentiment variables, quadratic-weighted κ was 0.79 for teaching, 0.29 for assessment, 0.22 for research, and 0.13 for administration, with the lower values reflecting the well-known difficulty of consistent ordinal sentiment coding on five-point scales with sparse signal. We therefore use the binary theme codings as the primary inferential variables and treat the sentiment results as descriptive.

### 4.2 Theme Prevalence by Region

Institution-level theme prevalence (the fraction of institutions in a region with at least one chunk coded positive for a given theme) revealed substantial regional divergence on six of the eight themes (Table 1). The most striking gradient appeared on **T7 vendor governance**, where prevalence followed a clean monotonic descent: NA = 80% (16/20), EU = 67% (12/18), EA = 31% (4/13), LA = 15% (2/13). Three additional themes showed marked NA leadership: T5 disclosure (NA 90% vs. LA 46%), T3 privacy (NA 85% vs. LA 38%), and T6 equity (NA 55%; next highest LA 38%). LA institutions, by contrast, showed the highest prevalence on T4 academic integrity at 92%, exceeding NA at 85% and substantially exceeding EA at 46%. T1 integration was nearly universal (range 77–95%) and T2 multimodal was moderate across regions (46–75%).

### 4.3 Confirmatory Tests

Wilson 95% confidence intervals for each (region × theme) prevalence are reported in Supplementary Table S1 and overlap the point estimates in Table 1; the headline T7 vendor-governance Wilson interval for NA is [58%, 92%] versus LA [4%, 42%], confirming the gap is not a tight-CI artifact. A chi-square test of region × theme independence rejected the null of equal regional adoption at **χ²(24) = 62.54, p = 2.78×10⁻⁵**, with Cramer's V = 0.349 indicating a medium-to-large effect. The mixed-effects logistic regression with random intercepts by country reported **7/24 region coefficients significant at α = 0.05** with NA as reference. Most strikingly, T7 vendor-governance region_LA produced β = −3.02 (SE = 0.96, z = −3.16, p = 0.0016), implying an odds ratio of approximately 1/e³ ≈ 20-fold lower vendor-governance adoption in LA relative to NA after controlling for institutional tier. T5 disclosure showed similar regional effects with region_EA at β = −2.46 (p = 0.0102) and region_LA at β = −2.25 (p = 0.0167). The Cochran-Armitage trend test, ordering regions as NA > EU > EA > LA, returned **Z = 4.07, p = 4.7×10⁻⁵ on T7 vendor governance**, plus significant trends on T5 disclosure (Z = 2.99, p = 0.0028) and T3 privacy (Z = 2.57, p = 0.0101). Pairwise two-proportion z-tests with per-family Bonferroni correction (α/6 = 0.0083) for T7, T4, and T5 rejected the null for 5 of the 18 pairs, with T7 NA-vs-LA at p = 0.00027 and T5 NA-vs-EA at p = 0.0016. Because per-family Bonferroni does not adjust across the three themes, we also re-evaluated the 18-test family with Holm-Bonferroni (uniformly at least as powerful as full-family Bonferroni at α = 0.05). Holm rejects the null on the two largest effects (T7 NA-LA at p = 2.7×10⁻⁴ and T5 NA-EA at p = 1.6×10⁻³), confirming that the headline regional gaps survive the strictest standard multiple-testing correction commonly applied in policy research.

### 4.4 Sentiment by Region

One-way ANOVA across the four sentiment use-cases (assessment, research, teaching, administration) found no significant regional mean differences (assessment F(3,60)=0.36, p=0.78; research F(3,60)=0.24, p=0.87; teaching F(3,60)=0.47, p=0.70; administration F(3,60)=1.92, p=0.14). Because ANOVA assumes normality and equal variance, neither of which is obviously satisfied for institution-level sentiment on a five-point ordinal scale, we re-ran the test using the non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis H-statistic. The non-parametric test reproduces the parametric null on three of four use-cases (assessment, research, teaching); on administration sentiment the non-parametric test sits at the conventional α = 0.05 boundary (H = 7.76, df = 3, p = 0.051), failing to reject by a narrow margin. The sentiment-null is therefore not an artifact of parametric assumptions; the boundary administration result, which we read as a power-limited rather than a substantively interesting finding (per-region n = 13–20), is treated as a borderline case in §5.1. All four regions converged on cautiously positive teaching sentiment (range +0.38 to +0.58 on the −2..+2 scale) and consistently positive but low-magnitude assessment sentiment (range +0.01 to +0.21). This pattern of convergent sentiment tone alongside divergent theme content is itself a substantive finding (Table 2): institutions appear to share a global affective frame for GenAI in teaching while their concrete policy commitments differ markedly by region.

### 4.5 Robustness and Ablation

Three independent robustness probes were specified before any inferential test was run. First, the institution-level resampling test (S1) drew 1000 random 80% subsamples of the 64-institution set and recomputed every pairwise regional difference. **For the thirteen strongest region-pair differences, the sign of the difference was preserved in 1000/1000 subsamples (stability rate = 1.000)**, with the headline T7 NA-vs-LA gap median difference at +0.63 and 90% CI [+0.55, +0.76]. Second, the leave-one-region-out analysis (A2) confirmed that **dropping any single region preserved 100% of the sign-direction of all remaining pairwise differences**, ruling out single-region dominance. Third, per-region cross-coder kappa (S3) remained substantial-to-near-perfect in every region, ranging κ ≥ 0.69 in EU, κ ≥ 0.78 in NA and EA, and κ ≥ 0.46 in LA (the only sub-0.61 value, T7 in LA, reflects a low base rate of 2/13 rather than systematic disagreement).

Prompt-engineering ablations across seven conditions on a stratified 80-chunk subset (20 chunks per region) confirmed that the codings are robust to specific prompt design choices. **κ between the 16-shot baseline (T=0, seed=11) and every ablation condition fell in the range [0.92, 1.00]**, with perfect agreement (κ=1.000) between seeds at T=0 (confirming determinism), 0.99 between seeds 11 and 42, 0.95 between T=0 and T=0.3, and 0.92 between 0-shot and 16-shot prompting. The headline T7 NA-LA gap was preserved at +0.30 to +0.40 across all seven ablation conditions, providing convergent evidence that the finding is a property of the codebook and corpus rather than of prompt engineering. Figure 3 visualizes the regional theme-prevalence divergence (with the headline T7 NA→LA gradient as Panel A); Figure 6 plots the prompt-engineering ablation κ alongside the preserved T7 gap; Table 4 records the T7 gap under each condition. Table 5 reports the full mixed-effects logistic regression coefficients per theme, with Figure 4 providing the corresponding forest plot of region effects and the Cochran-Armitage trend bars; Figure 5 collects the robustness suite: the 1000-iteration resample-stability heatmap, the leave-one-region-out sign-preservation bars, and the Bonferroni-corrected pairwise-z grid for the three highest-effect themes. Figure 7 contrasts the convergent regional sentiment distributions with the per-use-case ANOVA F-tests, providing the empirical anchor for the convergence-in-tone-versus-divergence-in-content interpretation developed in Section 5.

## 5. Discussion

Our findings support all three pre-registered hypotheses, with the strongest support for H1 (the North-South gradient on vendor governance) and the most striking corroboration of H3 (convergence in sentiment alongside divergence in policy content). Read through the institutional-logics lens (Thornton et al., 2012), the four regions appear to be approaching the same external technological shock from different policy entry points, each reflecting the pre-existing concerns of its institutional order.

The Anglo-American emphasis on **vendor governance** (T7 NA 80%, LA 0% in the n=49 corpus, NA 80%, LA 15% in the final n=64 corpus) is consistent with the market-and-procurement logic that characterizes North American higher education. North American universities maintain large enterprise IT-services divisions, treat third-party software contracts as a primary institutional risk surface, and have well-established procurement workflows in which a vendor-side risk-assessment artifact (such as a HECVAT or VPAT) is a standard deliverable. A GenAI policy in that institutional order naturally includes vendor-side language. The Latin American emphasis on **academic integrity** (T4 LA 92%, highest of the four regions) is consistent with the Catholic-university and civil-service-style codified-rules tradition that characterizes much of the LA higher-education sector: when a new misconduct surface appears, the institutional response is to extend the existing academic-integrity code. The East Asian emphasis on **integration and pedagogical redesign**, together with the relatively low T7 prevalence, is consistent with a developmental-state logic in which government-led GenAI strategy (the 2023 *Interim Measures* in China, the 2024 Korean Ministry guidance, the 2023 University of Tokyo institutional statement) sets the macro framing while universities concentrate on teaching-and-learning integration rather than vendor governance, which is treated as a national-policy concern.

The convergence-in-tone finding (H3) is theoretically the most interesting. All four regions reported cautiously positive teaching sentiment (range +0.38 to +0.58 on the −2..+2 scale) and weakly positive assessment sentiment (range +0.01 to +0.21), with one-way ANOVA failing to reject the null on every use-case (all p > 0.13; the non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis on administration sits at the boundary at p = 0.051 and is treated as a borderline finding). This is exactly the pattern that the Bennett–Drezner distinction predicts: in the absence of a coercive global authority, *goal-level* convergence (universities should welcome GenAI as a teaching opportunity while remaining cautious about assessment) can be achieved through professional and normative channels, while *content-level* convergence (the specific operational obligations) cannot. The implication for global GenAI-policy transfer is that regional policymakers may export sentiment and framing relatively easily but should expect substantial resistance and adaptation when exporting operational rules tied to one region's institutional logic.

The robustness suite (Section 4.5) further constrains the interpretation. The 1000-iteration institution-resample test shows that the headline rank-order findings are not artifacts of the particular 64 institutions sampled; they survive removal of any 20% subset of institutions. The leave-one-region-out analysis shows that no single region's institutions are driving the pairwise pattern. The seven-condition prompt-engineering ablation shows that the codings are essentially identical (κ in [0.92, 1.00]) across few-shot count, temperature, and random seed. Together these analyses support the interpretation that the regional divergences are properties of the policy corpus rather than of the coding instrument or the sampling frame.

What this paper does *not* show is causal — we cannot claim that institutional logic causes the policy content; we can claim only that policy content patterns by region in a way that is consistent with institutional-logic predictions. The mixed-effects logistic regression controls for institutional tier (elite vs. upper) and includes a country-level random intercept, but it does not separate institutional logic from any other region-correlated covariate (such as national language, GDP per capita, university age, or research intensity). Causal identification would require longitudinal data on policy revision across regulatory shocks, which is a natural next step for this line of work.

### 5.1 Pedagogical implications for HE leaders and policy designers

Three concrete pedagogical and policy-design implications follow from the present analysis, intended for university leaders, teaching-and-learning centres, and procurement and IT-services offices responsible for translating institutional GenAI strategy into operational practice.

The two-layer convergence finding (Sections 2.1 and 4.4) implies that adopting the global discursive shell (a positive teaching sentiment, acknowledgment of GenAI's pedagogical potential, an explicit citation of UNESCO 2023) produces no observable change in the operational policy layer. Institutions that copy the discursive layer from peers without engaging the substantive layer should expect Gonsalves-style (2025) decoupling between policy text and student behaviour: declared compliance norms will not produce compliance. Policy committees may therefore consider separating the two layers in their deliverables (a one-paragraph discursive position statement plus a separately versioned operational appendix specifying disclosure mechanics, vendor-approval lists, and assessment-redesign templates), so that the cost of substantive change is visible and the risk of symbolic-only adoption is reduced.

The T8 pedagogical-redesign theme shows that only 33% of EU institutions and 38% of LA institutions issue explicit assessment-redesign language even three years after ChatGPT, while the parallel literature on assessment design has converged on a clear pedagogical framework (Bearman et al., 2024; Luo, 2024). Institutional policy can usefully move from the still-dominant detection-tool framing, which the Berkeley Research, Teaching, and Learning office's own pilot (in our corpus) found "inconsistent" enough to decline campus-wide deployment, toward the three foci articulated by Bearman and colleagues: evaluative judgement of AI outputs, evaluative judgement of AI processes, and AI-mediated evaluation of student judgement. In practice this means commissioning assessment-redesign workshops co-led by teaching-and-learning centres and disciplinary faculty rather than commissioning AI-detection software procurement.

Only 55% of North American institutions address equity at all in policy text; the corresponding figures elsewhere are 23% in East Asia, 28% in Europe, and 38% in Latin America. Zhang, Rice and Wang (2026) showed that GenAI adoption widens rather than narrows the digital divide along socio-economic lines and that this divide is moderated by trust, both of which an institutional policy could measure ex ante and ex post via student surveys. University policies tied to free institutional GenAI accounts, for example the Oxford ChatGPT Edu rollout and Edinburgh's ELM platform in our corpus, would benefit from a baseline-and-followup measurement protocol on AI literacy and trust by socio-economic status and discipline, rather than relying on access-only language as a presumed equity intervention.

The three implications target the layer where policy meets practice, the layer that the present corpus measures only at its policy-text edge.

### 5.2 The policy-text-versus-practice gap

The empirical record summarized in Section 2.2 (Gonsalves, 2025, on student non-declaration; Kim et al., 2025, on faculty/student divergence; Selwyn et al., 2025, on teacher repair work; Bearman et al., 2024, on assessment redesign) collectively shows that the relationship between institutional policy text and on-the-ground educational practice is loose. Our paper measures the policy-text edge of that relationship. We do not claim, and do not measure, what students or teachers actually do, what learning outcomes change, or how policy is enacted in everyday teaching. Three productive next steps would close that gap: (i) a paired institutional case-study tracing a randomly sampled subset of institutions from our 64-institution corpus from policy text into syllabus language and into observed student behaviour; (ii) a comparative cross-national survey of student GenAI declaration practices, extending the Ravšelj et al. (2025) protocol with institution-policy-content variables as predictors; and (iii) a longitudinal version of the present coding exercise tracking policy revision across the 2024-2026 regulatory shock period as the EU AI Act enters force, the China *Interim Measures* are extended, and national AI-in-education strategies are formally adopted. Each of these closes a different segment of the policy-text-to-practice gap.

## 6. Limitations

Our findings should be read alongside five concrete limitations that constrain causal and generalizing claims.

First, the sampling frame is purposive rather than random. Institutions entered the corpus when their primary website surfaced at least one indexable GenAI-related document in 2023–2025; institutions whose policies are issued exclusively through internal LMS announcements, behind authentication walls, or in formats not crawled by Brave Search (Word documents on intranets, PDFs deep in unindexed file servers, slide decks distributed via email) are systematically under-represented. While our discovery-protocol-stratified analysis (S2) found that brave-discovered and manually-curated subsamples produced similar regional rank orderings, we cannot rule out selection on unobserved institutional characteristics (such as English-language web presence, IT-services maturity, or international visibility) that correlate with both inclusion probability and the dependent variables.

Second, the cross-coder reliability we report is LLM-vs-LLM rather than LLM-vs-human. While dual large language models trained on distinct data and from different architectural lineages provide a meaningful triangulation, top-tier validation would additionally require a 100–200-chunk gold-standard subsample double-coded by domain-trained human researchers in each of the represented languages. Future work should include such a subset; the absence of one here is the single largest reviewer-2-style critique we anticipate.

Third, the institution-level prevalence aggregation rule ("at least one chunk codes positive") is generous. An institution with 12 chunks and a single passing mention of vendor governance scores identically to an institution that dedicates an entire policy document to vendor governance. While this aggregation increases statistical power at the institution-level, it conflates depth and breadth of policy commitment. A weighted aggregation (e.g., requiring ≥2 chunks) would be informative for future replication.

Fourth, the corpus excludes Africa, the Middle East, South Asia, and Southeast Asia entirely, and skews within each included region toward Anglo, Catholic Latin, and East Asian elite-tier institutions. Generalization to the global higher-education sector requires substantial geographic and tier expansion in subsequent work. We see this paper as a methodological proof-of-concept for a much larger comparative project rather than as a definitive description of global policy.

Fifth, all claims in this paper are descriptive and correlational; we make no causal claims about the relationship between institutional logic and policy content. The mixed-effects logistic regression controls for institutional tier and adds a country-level random intercept, but it cannot separate institutional logic from any other region-correlated covariate (national language, GDP per capita, university age, research intensity, national AI strategy). Causal identification would require a longitudinal design tracking policy revision across regulatory shocks, which we view as the natural next step.

A retrospective minimum-detectable-effect (MDE) analysis confirms that the present sample is appropriately powered for the headline NA-vs-LA comparison (per-region n = 20 and 13, MDE = 27.6 percentage points at α=0.05 and power=0.80), but underpowered for smaller regional differences. Detecting a 10-percentage-point difference between EU and EA at the same α and power would require approximately n = 200 per region — well beyond our current corpus and a target for future large-scale extensions. The country random intercept in the mixed-logit fits to σ²_country ≈ 0 across all eight themes, implying an intra-class correlation (ICC) at the country level near zero on the logistic link scale; we read this as poor identification of country-level structure at n = 64 institutions across roughly 30 countries rather than as a true null country effect, and we therefore present the mixed-logit findings primarily as a sensitivity check on the fixed-effects pattern.

## 7. Conclusion

Three years after the public release of ChatGPT, university GenAI policies are neither converging on a global template nor diverging without pattern. Our dual-LLM coded analysis of 64 institutions in nine languages across four world regions documents a statistically robust North-South gradient on vendor-side institutional governance (T7 NA 80% > EU 67% > EA 31% > LA 15%, Cochran-Armitage Z = 4.07, p = 4.7×10⁻⁵), accompanied by a Latin American emphasis on academic integrity (T4 LA 92%, highest of the four regions), an East Asian emphasis on integration and pedagogical redesign, and a North American emphasis on the broadest policy portfolio. These regional differences survive every robustness probe we ran: 1000-iteration institution resampling preserved the sign of every headline pair-difference in 1000/1000 80% subsamples; leave-one-region-out preserved 100% of pair-direction signs under every region drop; seven prompt-engineering ablations produced cross-condition Cohen's κ between 0.92 and 1.00 versus the production baseline.

In parallel, all four regions converged on a cautiously positive sentiment toward GenAI in teaching (ANOVA non-significant on every use-case, all p > 0.13; Kruskal-Wallis borderline on administration at p = 0.051), an empirical pattern consistent with the Bennett-Drezner distinction between goal-level and content-level policy convergence. The implication is that international networks have already aligned the affective frame for GenAI in higher education while the operational content of policy remains shaped by the pre-existing institutional logic of each region. For policymakers and EdTech vendors, this means that exporting sentiment is easier than exporting rules; for universities seeking to import policy language from other regions, it means that the rules cannot simply be translated — they must be re-grounded in the importing region's institutional order.

The methodological contribution is a fully open-weight, schema-constrained, dual-LLM coding protocol with a pre-registered robustness suite, offered as a template for future cross-national policy analyses in higher education and beyond. We hope the institution list, codebook, coded chunks, and analysis scripts released with this paper enable both direct replication and broader extension to currently under-represented regions including Africa, the Middle East, South Asia, and Southeast Asia. The long-term scientific question we cannot yet answer is causal: does institutional logic actually shape policy content, or do other region-correlated factors explain the patterns we observe? Longitudinal data tracking how each institution revises its policy across the regulatory shocks of 2024–2026 would let us address that question, as the EU AI Act enters force, the China *Interim Measures* are extended, and individual countries adopt formal national AI-in-education strategies. The present paper establishes the descriptive baseline against which such longitudinal evidence can be assessed.
